BROWN DWARFS


I am working with Mike Liu and Gene Magnier on a search for brown dwarfs in large survey catalogs: Pan-STARRS, WISE, 2MASS, and UKIDSS. A brown dwarf is a ball of mostly gas that is larger than a planet, but smaller than a star. A brown dwarf gets hot when it forms -- as hot as
3000 K -- but not hot enough to start a nuclear fusion reaction in its core, the way a star does. So once it has formed, a brown dwarf simply cools down slowly, taking billions of years to get cold. You can think of a brown dwarf as a super-sized Jupiter that's warm or hot, but is cooling off like an ember after a fire.

Brown dwarfs are much, much fainter than stars, but they do glow, and we can see them with telescopes if they aren't too far away. Because they are cooler than stars, their light is mostly infrared radiation. They are tricky to find, and the first unambiguous discovery only came in 1995. Today, we know of well over a thousand brown dwarfs. There are surely many more out there to discover!


My first project involved identification of nearby brown dwarfs (within 25 parsecs of the Sun) whose temperatures are about 900 - 1300 K, which are hard to distinguish from other types of stars. These are called L/T transition dwarfs, and their atmospheres have very interesting chemical reactions that astronomers are trying to understand. Astronomers have found fewer L/T transition dwarfs than other kinds of brown dwarfs, so our new discoveries will enhance the study of these elusive and mysterious objects.


My next project, which is my PhD thesis, widens the scope to search for all ultracool dwarfs with spectral types L0 through T6 within 25 parsecs over two thirds of the sky. I am measuring precise parallax distances to these objects, and investigating whether they are single, binary, or multiple systems using high-resolution imaging. This volume-limited sample will present the first comprehensive picture of the local ultracool field population. I will measure the luminosity function, mass function, multiplicity, and kinematics, and use population synthesis modeling to extract the low-mass IMF and solar-neighborhood birth history with unparalleled accuracy. I will characterize the puzzling diversity of near-infrared colors among L dwarfs and model how brown dwarfs cool through the L/T transition. Finally, I will be able to place rare ultracool discoveries in a well-defined demographic context, including the extremely red L dwarfs that appear to be our best field analogs for directly-imaged giant exoplanets.

PROTOPLANETARY DISKS


I have also worked with Jonathan Williams on the gas content in protoplanetary disks, which are a natural result of the star formation process. A star forms when a large cloud of interstellar gas (mostly hydrogen) and dust collapses in on itself. As the collapse happens, most of the gas and dust packs into the middle of the cloud -- the core. When the core becomes dense and hot enough, the hydrogen begins to burn via nuclear fusion, and a new star is born. This process can take about a million years.


The little bit of material that doesn't fall into the core flattens into a rotating disk of gas and dust, much like a spinning pizza dough spreads out and flattens. Within about 10 million years (sometimes less), material in this disk will eventually go to one of three places: it can fall onto the new star, it can be blown out into interstellar space by radiation from the new star, or it can clump into larger objects like comets, asteroids, and planets. So, protoplanetary disks are the birthplaces of planets. The Earth and the other planets in our Solar System must have formed in a disk like this.


We know that in interstellar clouds (pre-star), the gas-to-dust mass ratio is about 100:1, i.e. for every 100 g of gas, there's 1 g of dust. So a new protoplanetary disk will probably have this same gas-to-dust ratio. We also know that 10 million years later, whatever remains of the disk may have some dust and rocky material, but the gas is gone. So this means that giant planets like Jupiter and Neptune, which are mostly hydrogen gas, must form in at most 10 million years. After that, there's no more gas around their host star to build them up.


We know little about how the gas disappears from the disk. Slowly and gradually? All at once, at some critical point in time? Is the process related to the size and shape of the disk, or the temperature of the star? These are questions my project aimed to shed light on.


We measured the emission from different isotopologues of carbon monoxide (CO) in protoplanetary disks, as a way to estimate how much gas is in each disk. We used the Smithsonian Submillimeter Array to measure the CO isotoplogue lines at 1.3 mm. We also developed a grid of model disks by varying various parameters (disk geometry and structure, star properties, and chemical properties of CO), and then calculating the CO line strengths we expect to observe from each model. We determined that these CO lines can constrain the mass of disks to within a factor of three, a significant improvement over previous assumptions. We also determined that disks in Taurus appear to have gas-to-dust ratios well below 100:1, despite their young age of only 1-2 million years. This suggests that the gas may begin disappearing early in the lives of protoplanetary disks, giving giant planets a relatively short time to form.

 

My research interests include brown dwarfs in the solar neighborhood, protoplanetary disks, planet and star formation, and exoplanets.
